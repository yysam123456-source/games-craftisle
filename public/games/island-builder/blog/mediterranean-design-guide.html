Mediterranean Architecture Design Guide

Published May 21, 2026 ¡¤ 6 min read

There's something magical about Mediterranean island architecture. The whitewashed cubes clinging to hillsides, the cobalt blue domes against an azure sky, the narrow winding paths that seem to lead everywhere and nowhere. This guide will help you understand the design principles behind Greek island villages and apply them in the Mykonos Island Builder.

The White Cube: Why Everything Is Whitewashed

Walk through any Cycladic village and you'll notice: nearly everything is white. This isn't just aesthetic ¡ª it's practical. White lime wash reflects the harsh Mediterranean sun, keeping interiors cool. It's also antibacterial, and historically, it was cheap and readily available.

In the builder: Use White Stone tiles around your village center. Place Cube Houses, Two-Story buildings, and Villas close together to create the iconic "sugar cube" cluster effect.

The Blue Accent: More Than Just Pretty

The famous blue doors, windows, and domes of Greek islands have practical origins too. A cleaning agent called "loulaki" (a blue-tinted powder) was mixed into the lime wash. Fishermen painted their boats blue, and the tradition spread to architecture. The blue-and-white palette is now legally protected in some Cycladic villages.

In the builder: Add Blue Railings along paths and between buildings. The Tower Chapel and Main Chapel feature blue domes that anchor your village visually.

Organic Layout: No Grid, No Problem

Mediterranean villages weren't planned ¡ª they grew. Buildings cluster together for shade and wind protection. Streets narrow to a single-person width, then suddenly open into a sun-drenched plaza. The result is a maze-like charm that planned cities can't replicate.

In the builder: Avoid straight lines. Cluster buildings irregularly. Use Low Walls and Corner Walls to create enclosure. Place an Archway as a gateway between districts.

Flat Roofs and Terrace Life

Flat roofs serve as additional living space in Mediterranean homes ¡ª for drying laundry, storing goods, or simply enjoying the evening breeze. Terraces are where life happens.

In the builder: Place Terracotta Pots, Water Buckets, and Benches on and around buildings. The Terrace House and Pergola House explicitly reference this outdoor-living tradition.

Churches: The Heart of Every Village

Every Greek island village has at least one church, often many ¡ª small family chapels dot the landscape. The blue-domed church is the most photographed symbol of the Cyclades.

In the builder: The Main Chapel (3¡Á3) and Tower Chapel (2¡Á2) are your centerpiece buildings. Surround them with Lantern Posts, Cypress trees, and a Stone Lantern or two. Place the Altar nearby for a complete ecclesiastical complex.

Windmills: Harnessing the Meltemi

The iconic windmills of Mykonos were once the island's economic engine, grinding grain using the strong northern Meltemi wind. Today they're the most photographed landmarks after the churches.

In the builder: Place the Windmill on elevated terrain or at the edge of your village. It works best as a standalone feature ¡ª give it space to breathe, surrounded by grass or sand.
